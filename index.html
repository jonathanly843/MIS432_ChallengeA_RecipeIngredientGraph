Section 1

The Business Case

When a home cook runs out of an ingredient, they usually guess a substitute or give up. This system does something smarter: it maps ingredients as a network, connecting them through the cuisines they appear in and the flavors they share. When you ask "what can I use instead of lemon?", it doesn't just say "lime" — it explains that lime and lemon both appear in Mexican and Greek cooking, both taste acidic and bright, and scores every possible substitute so you see the strongest match first.

That ranked, explainable output is what turns a simple lookup into a recommendation engine. A grocery app, a meal-kit service, or a recipe platform could use this to reduce cart abandonment when items are out of stock, personalize menus around what's in season, or help users confidently cook with unfamiliar ingredients.

Like EveryCure (Lab 1)

A graph that finds hidden connections

EveryCure maps drugs, proteins, and diseases as a network, then scores drug-disease pairs by how many biological relationships connect them. This system does the same thing with ingredients, cuisines, and flavors. The AI doesn't "know" that lime substitutes for lemon — it discovers the connection by counting shared nodes in the graph. Both systems use the same logic: the more shared connections, the stronger the match.

Like Spotify (Lab 3)

Multi-ingredient taste profiling

Spotify builds a taste profile from your listening history — if you play three songs, it finds the fourth that fits all three. This system works the same way: pick three ingredients and it aggregates their combined cuisine and flavor connections to find what fits your whole selection, not just one item. That's collaborative filtering applied to your pantry instead of your playlist.
